**Title:** _Contextualized high-speed running and sprinting during English Premier League match-play with reference to possession, positional demands and opponent ranking. {Biology of Sport, 2025} (9 pages)._

**Authors:** _Ryland Morgans, Mauro Mandorino, Marco Beato, Ben Ryan, Piotr Zmijewski, Alexandre Moreira, Halil Ibrahim Ceylan, Rafael Oliveira. (Cardiff Metropolitan University, Brentford FC, Parma Calcio 1913, University of Rome, University of Suffolk, Jozef Pilsudski University, University of São Paulo, Ataturk University, CIDESD)_

**Key words:** _contextual variables_, _optical tracking_, _match performance_, _football_.

In this original research paper, Morgans et al. demonstrate that the factors influencing high-speed running (HSR) and sprint distance (SD) differ fundamentally depending on whether a team is in-possession (IP) or out-of-possession (OP). Specifically, during IP phases, playing position is the only significant predictor of HSR and SD (with wingers producing the highest values and centre-backs the lowest), whereas during OP phases, contextual factors (particularly opponent ranking difference and match location) become the dominant predictors, with HSR and SD decreasing as ranking difference increases and being higher in away matches. 

They do this by conducting a three-season retrospective study (2021–22 to 2023–24) of 31 EPL outfield players using Second Spectrum optical tracking data from all 114 league matches, classifying HSR and SD into IP and OP components, and applying a two-level multilevel regression analysis with playing position at Level 1 and contextual factors (ranking difference, match location) at Level 2, with player identity as a random effect. 

This is important to coaches, analysts and sports scientists because it provides a framework for contextualizing high-intensity running demands beyond aggregate match totals, revealing that tactical phase (IP vs. OP) requires fundamentally different analytical lenses and training considerations. 

This work would be useful when practitioners are designing position-specific training drills that account for possession phase, preparing tactically for opponents of varying quality, or creating individualized player profiles that reflect the contextual demands of both attacking and defensive phases of play.
