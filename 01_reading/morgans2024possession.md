In this original research paper, Morgans et al. demonstrate that both team formation (3-5-2 vs. 4-3-3) and ball possession percentage significantly influence match running performance in elite soccer, with players generally covering more total distance, high metabolic load (HML) distance, and performing more high-intensity efforts in a 3-5-2 formation compared to a 4-3-3. They also show that significant interaction effects exist between formation and possession, and that from a position-specific perspective, centre-backs and full-backs are the positions most affected by changes in formation and possession. 

They do this by conducting a two-year longitudinal study (2021–22 and 2022–23) of 27 EPL outfield players using 18 Hz GPS and Second Spectrum optical tracking (25 Hz), classifying possession into four categories via k-means clustering (very low to very high), and running two-way ANOVA across two formations and four possession levels for both team-level and position-specific analyses across 714 individual match data points. This is important to coaches, analysts and sports scientists because it provides evidence that tactical decisions about formation and possession strategy directly shape the physical demands placed on players, enabling more informed match preparation and training design. 

This work would be useful when coaches are selecting formations for specific opponents, designing position-specific training that accounts for tactical context, or when analysts are evaluating match running performance relative to the tactical approach employed.
