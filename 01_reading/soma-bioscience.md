# Notes on Soma Bioscience

# What is it? 

A biotechnology company that offers quantitative real-time salivary diagnostics. Their core product is lateral flow device built around two components:
- **Some Oral Fluid Collector**: a synthetic polymer swab with a volume adequacy indicator that collects 0.5ml of oral fluid 
- **Soma Cube Reader**: which reads the lateral flow test strips and provides quantitative results within about 12 minutes of sample collection. 

The biomarkers they measure are: 
- Salivary Cortisol (s-Cort)
- Salivary Immunoglobulin A (s-IgA)
- Salivary testosterone (s-T)
- Salivary alpha-amylase (s-AA)

## Salivary Cortisol 

Cortisol is the primary glucocorticoid stress hormone, produced by the adrenal glands bia the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. It's the body's main catabolic hormone, it mobilises energy stores, suppresses inflammation, and breaks down tissue to fuel the stress response. Salivary cortisol correlates strongly with blood serum cortisol, making it a reliable non-invasive proxy. Strong correlations have been reported between serum and salivary measures of cortisol during rest, following high-intensity exercise, and following football match play. 

**How it changes?**

- **Acutely after matches**: At 30 minutes post-match, salivary cortisol was significantly higher than at 60 minutes before kick-off, and remained elevated at 48 hours post-match (MD+2). This reflects the immediate catabolic stress response to high-intensity competition.

- **During congested schedules**: Sustained periods of increased load and high game density can produce 25-35% increases in cortisol levels longitudinally. Football match play typically induces acute increases in cortisol, signalling a catabolic state that can manifest for approximately 24-72 hours. 

- **Season-long trends**: An Italian Serie A study documented a positve variation of +35.65 in salivary cortisol across the competitive season, reflecting cumulative training and competition stress. 

- **Non-linear relationship with load**: Cortisol had a non-linear relationship with chronic high-speed running distance, meaning the cortisol response doesn't scale linearly with load - there are thresholds and inflection points. 

**What it tells you about readiness**: Chronically elevated cortisol indicates a player is in a sustained catabolic state - tissue is being broken down faster than it's being repaired. This signals inadequate recovery and increased injury risk. However, cortisol responses are inconsistent between individuals and are influenced by non-sport stressors (sleep, psychological state, time of day due to diurnal rhythm), which makes it unreliable as a standalone readiness marker. It's most useful in combination with testosterone. 

## Salivary Testosterone (s-T)

Testosterone is the primary anabolic hormone, produced via the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonodal (HPG) axis. It drives muscle protein synthesis, tissue repair, and recovery In a sports monitoring context, it represents the "rebuild" side of the equation. 

**How it changes**:

- **Acutely after matches**: Testosterone concentrations decreased by 30.6% after a match, and changes in testosterone correlated strongly with distance covered. Players who maintain higher testosterone levels during matches tend to cover more ground - this likely reflects both better fitness and less accumulated fatigue. 

-**Chronic load relationships**: Testosterone had non-linear relationships with chronic total distance (large effect), chronic high-metabolic load distance, and chronic high-speed running distance, along with a linear negative relationship with chronic sRPE. The non-linear relationships are critical - they suggest there's an optimal chronic loading zone where testosterone is maintained, but both under and over-loading can suppress it.

- **Season-long trends**: In the Italian Series A study, testosterone showed a positive variation of +27.3% across the season, suggesting adaptive increases in players are coping with the training programme.

**What it tells you about readiness**: Declining testosterone signals that anabolic capacity in compromised - the player's body is prioritising stress management over repair. When testosterone drops coincide with elevated cortisol, the player is in particularly poor recovery state. 

## Testosterone-to-Cortisol Ration (T:C)

This is the most commonly used composite indicator of anabolic-catabolic balance. It integrates both hormones into a single readiness signal. 

**How it changes?**

- **After match play**: The T:C ratio decreased by 64.2% after a football match reflecting an acute shift towards catabolism. 

- **Chronic load associations**: T:C had a non-linear relationship with chronic decelerations and chronic summated acceleration and deceleration load, with non-linear relationships typically indicating optimal hormonal responses at squad mean loads. This is a key finding - it means players at average training loads have the best hormonal balance, while those at extremes (very low or very high) show compromised T:C ratios. 

Since muscular recovery is augmented in anabolic environments, s-T, s-C and s-T:C are considered useful indicators of athletic readiness. 

**What it tells you about readiness**: A falling T:C ratio is the clearest single hormonal signal that a player is under-recovered. A greater T:C ratio has been associated with greater lower body power and improvements in other performance outcomes, possibly related to athletes possessing a favourable anabolic-to-catabolic balance and heightened psycho-physiological readiness. In practical terms: if a players T:C ratio drops below their individualised baselines threshold, that's a flag that they may need reduced loading, extra recovery, or potential rotation from the match-day squad. 

## s-IgA

IgA is the dominant antibody at mucosal surfaces (mouth, nose, respiratory tract, gut). It's the body's first line of defence against pathogens - it prevents viruses and bacteria from attaching to an penetrating mucosal tissues. It's secreted by the mucosal immune system and is regulated by both the sympathetic nervous system (SAM axis) and psychological stress. 

**How it changes**:

- **Acutely around matches**: Lower at 60 minutes before kick offs, likely reflecting anticipatory psychological stress. IgA then rose 30 minutes post-match and MD+2.

- **Congested fixture schedules**: A systematic review confirmed that lower values of IgA occurred after greater training load and congested periodss, and that low levels of IgA were correlated with higher rates of upper respiratory tract infection. **Morgans** has published early work showing a transient fall in s-IgA during the intense Christmas fixture period in EPL players. 

- **Youth players**: In elite youth soccer players during a congested match schedule of 7 matches in 7 days, s-IgA concentration decreased from the first to the last time point, with the highest value observed on the rest day. 

- **Short term vs. Long term sensitivity**: s-IgA measures might indicate short-term but not long-term stress balance within microcycles in football players. This is an important limitation - IgA is reactive to recent stressors but doesn't accumulate the way chronic hormonal changes do. 

**What it tells you about readiness**: Low s-IgA is the most consistent biomarker for **predicting illness risk.** Low levels of salivary IgA and a decline over a training period have identified athletes at risk of upper respiratory symptoms. The availability of point-of-care detection systems for salivary IgA pave the way for more systematic monitoring of immune status in athletes. Risk is elevated only when salivary IgA levels decrease by over 60% below an individual's healthy baseline values which underscore the importance of establishing player-specific baselines rather than using squad-wide thresholds. 

## Salivary Alpha-Amylase (s_AA)

Alpha-Amylase is a digestive enzyme secreted by the salivary glands, but it's primarily used as a marker of sympathetic nervous system (SNS) activation - essentially a proxy for "fight or flight" response. 

**How it changes**:

- s_AA responds to acute stressors including physical exercise, psychological stress, and sleep deprivation. Sleep deprivation affected proteins, peptides, and alpha-amylase levels. However, in football-specific contexts, the evidence is weaker than for the hormonal and IgA markers. 

**What it tells you about readiness**: s-AA provides a snapshot of autonomic nervous system activation, which can reflect acute psychophysical stress. But like IgA, acute load variables were not related to alpha-amylase changes, so it's less useful for load-readiness relationships. 

# Summary

**No single biomarker is sufficient on its own**. There is evidence to support the use of these measures as part of an individualised multivariate player monitoring system in elite-level professional football players. 

The practical readiness framework emerging from the literature looks something like: 

- s-Iga monitors **illness risk**, T:C ratio monitors **recovery status**, and cortisol in isolation monitors **acute stress response**. (how hard was today's session on this player?)

When combined with external load data, you get a far richer picture than either data stream provides alone. 

Key note is that the relationships are overwhelmingly **non linear, and individual specific**. The existing frequentist analyses can identify that these relationships exist at a group level, but they can't produce the kind of individualised, probabilistic readiness score that a practitioner actually needs.
