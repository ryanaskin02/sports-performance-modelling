**Title:** _Positional training demands in the English Premier League and English Championship. A longitudinal study across consecutive seasons. {Biology of Sport, 2025} (8 pages)._

**Authors:** _Ryland Morgans, Ibrahim H. Ceylan, John Radnor, Ben Ryan, Matthew King, Piotr Zmijewski, Rafael Oliveira. (Cardiff Metropolitan University, Ataturk University, Brentford FC, Jozef Pilsudski University, Instituto Politecnico de Santarem)_

**Key words:** _Global Positioning System_, _training load_, _elite European soccer players_, _competitive level_, _seasonal trends_.

In this original research paper, Morgans et al. demonstrate that training loads in the English Premier League (EPL) are significantly higher than in the English Championship League (ECL) for most GPS-derived metrics, with the notable exception of sprint distance and sprint efforts which were marginally higher in the ECL. They also show that positional differences exist, with centre midfielders covering the most distance per minute and full-backs producing the highest high-speed running, sprint, acceleration and deceleration values. 

They do this by conducting a four-year longitudinal study (2019–20 to 2022–23) tracking 46 first-team outfield players from the same club across both leagues using 18 Hz GPS technology (Statsports Apex), collecting 65,219 individual training data points from 840 team training days, and analyzing relative distances per minute via two-way ANOVA across five playing positions and two league levels. 

This is important to coaches, sports scientists and performance staff because understanding league-level and position-specific training demands helps inform the design of training programmes, manage workloads, and prepare ECL teams for the increased physical demands of promotion to the EPL. 

This work would be useful when coaches and practitioners are designing position-specific conditioning drills, managing training load across a season, or benchmarking training intensity against EPL and ECL reference values.
