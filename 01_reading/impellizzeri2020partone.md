## 1. What is this document saying?

This is a provocation piece arguing that the field's belief in its ability to control injury risk by manipulating training load metrics is largely an illusion. The core claim: 
- No strong evidence supports the quantitative use of TL data to manipulate future training for the purpose of preventing injury. The associations found in the literature are descriptive at best, predictive in a few cases, but never causal, and without established causation, manipulating a variable (TL) cannot be assumed to change the outcome (injury). 

They use the ice cream / shark attacks analogy: ice cream sales predict shark attacks, but banning ice cream won't reduce them. The paper reframes TL monitoring as belonging to the implementation phase of training rather than the planning phase, and argues the field has conflated the two. Recommendations like "don't do too much too soon" are sensible but come from generic training principles, not from TL-injury studies — and practitioners have been applying these principles for decades without needing ACWR or similar metrics.

## 2. How do they show this? 

Not empirically. This is a conceptual commentary with three frameworks presented as figures. First, the training process framework. Second, a simple process-control model showing TL monitoring as comparing planned vs. completed load and taking corrective action on deviations. Third, an operational framework integrating training planning and monitoring, distinguishing Path A (adjusting load based on training principles and athlete response) from Path B (adjusting load based on injury risk derived from TL-injury studies). 

They also reference the Kalkhoven et al. (2020) conceptual model for stress/strain-related injuries, arguing that injury prevention should target tissue-specific strength and tissue-specific mechanical loading, which current TL metrics cannot capture. The TRIPP framework (Finch 2006) is invoked to show that the field has jumped from stage 1 (surveillance) straight to stage 3 (intervention) without properly completing stage 2 (understanding injury aetiology and causal mechanisms). Part 2 of the series (not included here) addresses the specific methodological problems with ACWR and related TL-injury studies.
