## 1. What is it and what does it say? 

 Essentially a broader, more international follow-up to Akenhead & Nassis (2016), but with two added dimensions. It measures club culture (belief in evidence-based practice) and club financial resources (via Transfermarkt market values), then tests whether these predict how extensively monitoring is actually implemented. Main findings: 
 
 - Data collection is extensive across the board. External load collected most, internal load and training outcome data also widespread but less so. However, analysis is largely stuck at the descriptive level (reporting what happened) rather than diagnostic (modelling relationships between load and outcomes). 
 
 - Sport science staff own the collection and analysis pipeline; coaches and medical staff only really engage at the discussion/application end. The key new contribution: both a stronger evidence-based-practice (EBP) culture and greater financial resources independently predict more diverse data collection, more advanced analysis methods, and greater employment of specialist roles like dedicated sport scientists and performance managers. Clubs with high resources also tend to have stronger EBP beliefs, so the two factors reinforce each other.

## 2. How does it do this? 

Online survey (38 closed-ended questions, mix of multiple choice and 5-point Likert scales) distributed January–September 2019 to senior clubs in the top leagues of the 25 highest-ranked and second leagues of the 10 highest-ranked UEFA federations — 197 clubs invited, 99 responded (50% response rate), covering 15 federations. Much broader and more representative than Akenhead & Nassis's convenience sample of 41 clubs with established sport science departments. EBP belief quantified via five Likert items. Financial resources proxied by Transfermarkt squad market value. Clubs split into quartiles on each factor; Q1 vs Q4 comparisons used throughout to show gradient effects. Staff involvement mapped across five phases: collection $\rightarrow$ analysis $\rightarrow$ reporting $\rightarrow$ discussion $\rightarrow$ application. 

## 3. Why bother to do this in the first place? 

Akenhead & Nassis (2016) documented monitoring practices but only in English clubs or clubs that already had established sport science departments — so they likely overestimated what the broader elite football landscape actually looks like. This study corrects for that sampling bias by going pan-European. More importantly, previous papers flagged coach buy-in, limited resources, and poor analytical depth as barriers, but nobody had actually measured the structural factors (culture and money) that might explain why some clubs do this well and others don't. This paper provides the first evidence that the gap between "data-rich" and "insight-poor" clubs is at least partly explained by institutional culture and financial capacity and not just individual practitioner skill. 

## 4. Target Audience? 

Applied sport scientists and performance staff across European football.
