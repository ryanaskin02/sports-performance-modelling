## 1. What is this document and what does it say? 

This is a theoretical framework paper. Central argument: training load monitoring has been treating all load metrics as if they feed into the same adaptation process, but there are actually two distinct pathways

- physiological (metabolic/cardiorespiratory stress, think "engine") 
- biomechanical (mechanical stress on musculoskeletal tissues, think "suspension"). 

These have different drivers, different indicators, and crucially different recovery timescales. Biomechanical tissues (tendon, cartilage, bone) adapt slower than cardiorespiratory fitness, so periodisation that's optimised for physiological supercompensation could be systematically degrading musculoskeletal tissue without anyone noticing. The paper presents a dual-pathway schematic and argues practitioners need to separate the two when choosing metrics and planning load.


## 2. How do they show this? 

They complete a narrative review and conceptual synthesis. They take existing evidence from GPS validation, accelerometry (PlayerLoad™ etc.), tissue engineering, and musculoskeletal adaptation literature and reorganise it under the two pathways:

- External load: distance/speed/metabolic power $\rightarrow$ physiological side; GRFs/accelerometer cumulative loads → biomechanical side. 

- Internal load: HR, $\text{VO}_2$, TRIMP $\rightarrow$ physiological; joint contact forces, muscle-tendon forces, CK, soreness $\rightarrow$ biomechanical. 

RPE sits across both; they flag differential RPE (breathlessness vs. leg exertion) as a way to separate them. Key figure shows how mismatched recovery rates between pathways can cause progressive tissue weakening even when physiological adaptation looks fine. Practical examples of independent manipulation: aqua jogging, anti-gravity treadmills, HIT (high physiol / low biomech), small-sided games on smaller pitches (lower physiol / higher biomech due to more accelerations), sand sprinting.

## 3. Why bother doing this? 

Practitioners had 50+ monitoring variables with no conceptual structure for what each one actually tells you. Correlating PlayerLoad with HR is asking the wrong question if they sit on different pathways. The confusion was partly because nobody had cleanly articulated that biomechanical and physiological loads are separate constructs with separate adaptation mechanisms. Also a direct injury-prevention motivation: if biomechanical recovery is slower than physiological recovery, standard periodisation could look fine on all the usual dashboards while quietly accumulating mechanical damage. This explains some of the "unexplained" non-contact soft tissue injuries.

## 4. Who is the target audience? 

Broad applied audience — sport scientists, coaches, medical staff in running-based team sports. It is deliberately accessible. For researchers: design studies that are explicit about which pathway you're investigating and pick metrics accordingly. For practitioners: know which pathway each of your variables serves, and consider whether your microcycle accounts for slower biomechanical recovery, which is especially relevant during rehab and return-to-play.
